CSR refers to business practices that consider the social and environmental impact of company operations. It reflects the idea that corporations have responsibilities beyond maximizing shareholder profit.

Carroll's CSR Pyramid

Archie Carroll (1991) proposed a four-level pyramid to understand CSR responsibilities:

Level 4 - Philanthropic Responsibility (Top)

Companies contribute to society through voluntary charitable giving, community involvement, and social programs. Examples: donations to education, arts patronage, volunteer employee programs.

"Doing good" - Discretionary, going beyond legal/ethical obligations

Level 3 - Ethical Responsibility

Companies operate fairly and transparently, respecting moral principles not required by law. Examples: fair labor practices, environmental protection, honest marketing, corruption prevention.

"Being fair" - Moral obligations expected by society

Level 2 - Legal Responsibility

Companies comply with laws and regulations. Examples: environmental laws, labor standards, consumer protection, tax obligations, workplace safety.

"Following the rules" - Legal requirements

Level 1 - Economic Responsibility (Base)

The fundamental responsibility to produce goods/services efficiently and profitably. This is the foundation enabling all other responsibilities.

"Making profits" - Core business purpose

Note: Each level builds on previous levels. Economic viability is the foundation; CSR adds ethical and social considerations on top.

Real-world Application: Unilever's CSR Strategy

Economic: Profitable business model ensuring sustainability of operations

Legal: Compliance with all environmental and labor laws globally

Ethical: Sustainable sourcing commitments; female empowerment; water conservation

Philanthropic: Lipton tea farming sustainability program; WASH program (water, sanitation, hygiene)

Result: Market leadership while contributing to global development goals
