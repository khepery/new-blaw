United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

Adopted in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, the UN SDGs provide a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure peace and prosperity. All 193 UN Member States have committed to achieving these goals.

Key Features:

  • 17 interconnected global goals
  • 169 targets to achieve by 2030
  • 244 official indicators for measurement
  • Applies to all countries (developed and developing)

Paris Agreement (2015)

A landmark international treaty adopted under the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change, requiring countries to:

  • Limit global warming to well below 2Â°C (preferably 1.5Â°C)
  • Review and strengthen climate commitments every 5 years
  • Provide financial support to developing nations
  • Transition to clean energy systems

Australia's Commitment: Ratified in 2016; committed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 43% by 2030 (2005 baseline).

Brundtland Report (1987)

Published by the World Commission on Environment and Development, the Brundtland Report coined the term "Sustainable Development" and defined it as:

"Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

Australian Legislation & Frameworks

  • Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation (EPBC) Act 1999: Australia's national environmental law protecting matters of national environmental significance
  • Climate Change Act 2022: Establishes Australia's net-zero emissions target by 2050
  • Modern Slavery Act 2018: Requires large organizations to report on modern slavery risks
  • ASX Corporate Governance Principles: Recommendations for ESG disclosure and board oversight
  • Treasury ESG Guidance: Encourages voluntary ESG reporting by companies

The Brundtland Definition

Sustainability refers to development that satisfies present needs while preserving resources for future generations. It requires balancing:

  • Economic Growth: Creating wealth and employment
  • Environmental Protection: Preserving natural resources and ecosystems
  • Social Equity: Ensuring fair treatment and opportunity for all

The Three Pillars of Sustainability

ğŸŒ Environmental Pillar

Protecting natural resources, reducing pollution, mitigating climate change, and preserving biodiversity. Focus on renewable energy, waste reduction, and circular economy principles.

ğŸ‘¥ Social Pillar

Ensuring human well-being, fair labor practices, community development, and social justice. Includes health, education, poverty reduction, and labor rights.

ğŸ’° Economic Pillar

Building resilient economies that create prosperity without depleting natural capital. Focus on sustainable business models, green jobs, and long-term value creation.

The Goal: Find the intersection where all three pillars overlap - achieving sustainable development that is economically viable, environmentally responsible, and socially equitable.

ğŸ¢ Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) ä¼ä¸šç¤¾ä¼šè´£ä»» â–¼

CSR refers to business practices that consider the social and environmental impact of company operations. It reflects the idea that corporations have responsibilities beyond maximizing shareholder profit.

Carroll's CSR Pyramid

Archie Carroll (1991) proposed a four-level pyramid to understand CSR responsibilities:

Level 4 - Philanthropic Responsibility (Top)

Companies contribute to society through voluntary charitable giving, community involvement, and social programs. Examples: donations to education, arts patronage, volunteer employee programs.

"Doing good" - Discretionary, going beyond legal/ethical obligations

Level 3 - Ethical Responsibility

Companies operate fairly and transparently, respecting moral principles not required by law. Examples: fair labor practices, environmental protection, honest marketing, corruption prevention.

"Being fair" - Moral obligations expected by society

Level 2 - Legal Responsibility

Companies comply with laws and regulations. Examples: environmental laws, labor standards, consumer protection, tax obligations, workplace safety.

"Following the rules" - Legal requirements

Level 1 - Economic Responsibility (Base)

The fundamental responsibility to produce goods/services efficiently and profitably. This is the foundation enabling all other responsibilities.

"Making profits" - Core business purpose

Note: Each level builds on previous levels. Economic viability is the foundation; CSR adds ethical and social considerations on top.

Real-world Application: Unilever's CSR Strategy

Economic: Profitable business model ensuring sustainability of operations

Legal: Compliance with all environmental and labor laws globally

Ethical: Sustainable sourcing commitments; female empowerment; water conservation

Philanthropic: Lipton tea farming sustainability program; WASH program (water, sanitation, hygiene)

Result: Market leadership while contributing to global development goals

Freeman's Stakeholder Theory

Developed by R. Edward Freeman (1984), stakeholder theory argues that companies should create value for all stakeholders, not just shareholders. This contrasts with shareholder primacy which focuses solely on maximizing shareholder returns.

Core Principle: "A business is not an island; it exists within a network of relationships with parties that affect and are affected by its operations."

Primary Stakeholders

Those directly affected by company operations and critical to survival:

  • Shareholders/Investors: Provide capital and bear financial risk
  • Employees: Provide labor and expertise; depend on company for livelihoods
  • Customers: Purchase products/services; quality and safety depend on company
  • Suppliers/Business Partners: Provide materials and services; depend on company for revenue
  • Lenders/Creditors: Provide financing; depend on company's financial health

Secondary Stakeholders

Those affected indirectly by company operations but important for social legitimacy:

  • Local Communities: Affected by environmental impact, employment, social programs
  • Governments/Regulators: Set rules and collect taxes
  • Environmental Groups: Advocate for ecological protection
  • Media & Public: Influence company reputation and social license
  • Industry Associations: Set standards and best practices

Freeman's 5-Step Stakeholder Management Process

Step 1: Identify Stakeholders

Map all parties affected by or affecting the company. Consider primary and secondary stakeholders, their interests, and relative influence.

Step 2: Analyze Stakeholder Interests

Understand what each stakeholder group values, their expectations, concerns, and priorities. Conduct surveys, interviews, and focus groups.

Step 3: Define Company Values & Strategy

Establish company mission, values, and strategic objectives that reflect stakeholder input and create shared value.

Step 4: Engage & Communicate

Maintain transparent, ongoing dialogue with stakeholders. Report on performance, listen to feedback, and adjust strategies accordingly.

Step 5: Monitor & Measure

Track stakeholder satisfaction, company performance against commitments, and adjust practices as needed. Report regularly on progress.

Stakeholder Conflict Example: Mining Company

Shareholders: Want high profits and stock returns

Employees: Want safe working conditions and fair wages

Local Communities: Want environmental protection and economic benefits

Government: Wants tax revenue and environmental compliance

Solution (Stakeholder Approach): Company invests in safety, environment, community development, creating sustainable value for all parties

ğŸ“Š Triple Bottom Line: People, Planet, Profit ä¸‰é‡åº•çº¿ â–¼

The Triple Bottom Line (3Ps) framework, developed by John Elkington (1997), expands traditional corporate accounting to include environmental and social performance alongside financial performance.

1ï¸âƒ£ People (Social Aspect)

Measuring how the company positively impacts social well-being:

  • Employee Welfare: Fair wages, benefits, work-life balance, career development
  • Community Development: Local economic development, education programs, healthcare support
  • Labor Rights: Safe working conditions, freedom of association, no discrimination
  • Supply Chain Impact: Ethical sourcing, fair trade, supplier development
  • Customer Impact: Product safety, quality, transparency

Metrics: Employee satisfaction scores, wage levels, volunteer hours, community impact assessments

ğŸŒ Planet (Environmental Aspect)

Measuring the company's environmental footprint and contribution to ecological health:

  • Carbon Emissions: Direct emissions (Scope 1), energy-related (Scope 2), supply chain (Scope 3)
  • Resource Use: Water consumption, raw material depletion, energy efficiency
  • Waste Management: Waste reduction, recycling rates, hazardous waste handling
  • Pollution: Air quality, water quality, soil contamination
  • Biodiversity: Ecosystem protection, land restoration

Metrics: Carbon footprint, water usage, renewable energy %, waste diversion rate, environmental audit scores

ğŸ’° Profit (Economic Aspect)

Traditional financial performance alongside economic sustainability:

  • Financial Returns: Revenue growth, profitability, shareholder returns
  • Economic Sustainability: Long-term viability, risk management, resilience
  • Value Creation: Innovation, market share, competitive advantage
  • Cost Efficiency: Operational efficiency, resource optimization
  • Economic Contribution: Tax payments, employment creation, supplier spending

Metrics: EBITDA, ROE, EPS, debt-to-equity ratio, economic value added

Case Study: Interface Inc. (Carpet Manufacturing)

People: Established "evergreen" employee programs; 40% of workforce trained for high-skills positions; industry-leading safety record

Planet: "Mission Zero" - zero waste to landfill by 2020 (achieved); 96% renewable energy; eliminated virgin PVC

Profit: Achieved 26% reduction in GHG intensity while maintaining 7% average annual revenue growth; market leader in sustainable carpeting

Impact: Proven that sustainability drives profitability through innovation, efficiency, and market differentiation

Case Study: Walmart - Triple Bottom Line Integration

People: Wage improvements for 500,000+ employees; supplier diversity programs; community disaster relief

Planet: 2030 target: zero waste to landfills; 100% renewable energy; sustainable sourcing commitments

Profit: Reduced supply chain costs by $1B+ through sustainability initiatives; expanded market through sustainability-focused customers

Integration: Triple bottom line approach became competitive advantage

SDG Implementation by Businesses

Companies integrate SDGs into strategy by:

  • Identifying Relevant Goals: Determine which SDGs align with their industry, operations, and stakeholder needs
  • Setting Targets: Establish measurable targets for contribution to SDG targets
  • Integration: Embed SDGs into business strategy, products, and operations
  • Measurement: Track progress using SDG indicators and third-party verification
  • Reporting: Disclose SDG progress in sustainability/annual reports

Scenario 1: Tech Company Expansion

Situation: A large technology company plans to build a new manufacturing facility in a developing country. The facility will create 5,000 jobs but requires construction on biodiverse land and could increase local carbon emissions by 15%.

Using Freeman's Stakeholder Theory and Triple Bottom Line framework:

  • Identify all primary and secondary stakeholders
  • Analyze potential conflicts between stakeholder interests
  • Propose how the company can create shared value across People, Planet, and Profit
  • Suggest ESG measures to mitigate negative impacts
Scenario 2: CSR Responsibility Conflict

Situation: A fashion retailer discovers that one of its key suppliers uses child labor in production. The company must decide: maintain the supplier (supporting 2,000 jobs), work with the supplier to improve practices (costly and time-consuming), or terminate the relationship (immediate ESG benefit but job losses).

Using Carroll's CSR Pyramid:

  • Analyze this situation across all four levels of responsibility
  • Identify which stakeholders are most affected by each option
  • What does the company's legal obligation require?
  • What ethical and philanthropic responsibilities apply?
  • Develop a comprehensive solution incorporating all CSR levels
Scenario 3: ESG Performance vs. Short-term Profits

Situation: A mining company faces pressure from shareholders to maximize quarterly profits. However, investing in environmental remediation and indigenous community relations (aligned with ESG) would reduce short-term earnings but improve long-term sustainability and social license to operate.

Discussion Questions:

  • How do ESG investments align with sustainable value creation?
  • What arguments support ESG investment despite short-term cost?
  • How can the company communicate ESG value to shareholders?
  • What risks does the company face if ESG is neglected?
  • How do UN SDGs provide a framework for this decision?
Scenario 4: Australian Compliance & Sustainability

Situation: An Australian food manufacturer must comply with the Climate Change Act 2022 (net-zero by 2050) and Modern Slavery Act 2018. The company operates in emerging markets where environmental and labor standards are less stringent.

Questions:

  • Should Australian companies apply the same standards globally? Why or why not?
  • How do international frameworks (Paris Agreement, UN SDGs) guide this decision?
  • What stakeholder perspectives should be considered?
  • How can the company balance compliance costs with competitive positioning?
  • What role does governance play in ensuring consistent ESG standards?
ğŸ“š Key Terms & Glossary å…³é”®æœ¯è¯­å’Œè¯æ±‡è¡¨ â–¼

Brundtland Report: 1987 World Commission report that defined sustainable development as meeting present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet their own needs.

Carbon Footprint: Total greenhouse gas emissions produced directly and indirectly by an organization, product, or individual, typically measured in CO2 equivalents.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR): Business practices reflecting concern for social and environmental impact; commitment to behave ethically and contribute to sustainable development.

ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance): Framework for measuring corporate sustainability and ethical impact across environmental stewardship, social responsibility, and governance practices.

Greenwashing: Marketing communications that exaggerate environmental benefits or hide negative environmental practices to appear more sustainable than reality.

Materiality: In sustainability context, refers to ESG issues that significantly impact business performance and stakeholder value; used to prioritize disclosure.

Net Zero Emissions: State achieved when greenhouse gas emissions from human activities are balanced by emission removals from the atmosphere, typically targeting 2050.

Scope 1, 2, 3 Emissions: Categorization of greenhouse gas emissions: Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (from purchased energy), Scope 3 (value chain indirect).

Stakeholder: Any person or group affected by or affecting a company's operations, including employees, customers, suppliers, communities, governments, investors.

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): 17 interconnected global goals adopted by UN member states in 2015 to achieve a better future for all by 2030.

Triple Bottom Line (3Ps): Framework expanding corporate accounting to measure People (social), Planet (environmental), and Profit (economic) performance simultaneously.

Transparency & Disclosure: Practice of openly reporting on corporate ESG performance, impacts, and governance through sustainability reports and annual disclosures.
