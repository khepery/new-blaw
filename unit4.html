Contracts are fundamental to business operations and daily life. Understanding contract law helps businesses:

  • Understand their rights and obligations

üîë Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

For a contract to be legally valid and enforceable, it must contain these essential elements:

  1. Offer - A clear proposal made by one party (the offeror) to another
  2. Acceptance - Unconditional agreement to the terms of the offer
  3. Consideration - Something of value exchanged between the parties
  4. Intention to Create Legal Relations - Parties must intend the agreement to be legally binding
  5. Capacity - Parties must have legal capacity to enter into a contract
  6. Legality - The purpose must be legal and not against public policy

üíº Offer

Definition: An offer is a definite proposal made by one party (offeror) to another (offeree) indicating a willingness to enter into a contract on certain terms.

Requirements of a Valid Offer:

  • Certainty: Terms must be clear and definite
  • Communicated: Must be communicated to the offeree
  • Intention: Must demonstrate intention to be bound

üìù Example: Valid Offer

"I will sell you my laptop for $500. This offer remains open until Friday 5pm."

This is a valid offer because it clearly states what is being sold, the price, and when the offer expires.

‚ö†Ô∏è Offer vs. Invitation to Treat

Important Distinction: Not all statements are offers. An "invitation to treat" is merely an invitation for others to make an offer.

Examples of Invitations to Treat:

  • Advertisements (generally)
  • Goods displayed in shop windows
  • Items on shelves in supermarkets
  • Auctions (each bid is an offer)

‚úÖ Acceptance

Definition: Acceptance is the unconditional agreement to all the terms of the offer, creating a binding agreement between the parties.

Rules of Acceptance:

Key Requirements:

  1. Unconditional: Must accept all terms without variation
  2. Communicated: Must be communicated to the offeror
  3. By Authorized Person: Must be made by the person to whom the offer was made
  4. Match the Offer: Must correspond exactly to the terms of the offer
  5. Within Time Limit: Must be made while the offer is still open

üìù Example: Valid Acceptance

Offer: "I'll sell my car for $10,000 cash."

Valid Acceptance: "I accept. I'll buy your car for $10,000 cash."

Invalid Acceptance (Counter-offer): "I'll buy your car for $9,000." - This is a counter-offer, not acceptance.

‚öñÔ∏è Case Study: Hyde v Wrench (1840)

Facts: Wrench offered to sell his farm to Hyde for ¬£1,000. Hyde offered ¬£950, which Wrench rejected. Hyde then tried to accept the original ¬£1,000 offer.

Held: There was no contract. Hyde's counter-offer of ¬£950 destroyed the original offer, so it was no longer available to accept.

Principle: A counter-offer terminates the original offer.

Methods of Acceptance:

  • Express Acceptance: Oral or written communication
  • Implied Acceptance: Conduct indicating acceptance
  • Postal Rule: Acceptance is complete when letter is posted (if postal acceptance is contemplated)

ü§ù Formation of Agreement

Agreement = Offer + Acceptance

When a valid offer meets with valid acceptance, an agreement is formed. This is the foundation of contract formation.

When is Agreement Complete?

The moment of acceptance determines when the agreement (and potentially the contract) comes into existence. This is crucial for determining:

  • When parties become bound
  • Which terms apply
  • Whether revocation of offer was effective

‚ùå Common Mistakes in Agreement Formation

  • Assuming silence equals acceptance - Generally, silence cannot constitute acceptance
  • Confusing negotiation with offers - Many negotiations are just invitations to treat
  • Accepting after deadline - Acceptance must occur while offer is still open
  • Changing terms during acceptance - Any variation creates a counter-offer, not acceptance
